The Moonshot
Moonshot Paper · 2026

Remind:
A prosthetic
for the self

Dementia cannot yet be cured. We argue the loss of the person can be — and present the first memory prosthetic: prescription AI glasses that hold a human identity as the brain lets it go.

Team: Stepan Dovbush  ·  Prototype: remind — interactive system prototype  ·  Category: unconventional AI system / deep tech

01

The problem humanity misunderstood

For forty years, civilization has treated dementia as a biology problem: find the molecule, clear the plaques, slow the decline. The result is one of the most complete failures in modern science — more than 99% of Alzheimer's drug trials have failed, and the few approved treatments buy months of marginally slower decline at enormous cost.

We believe the failure begins with a misdiagnosis of the problem itself. Ask any family what dementia took from them and no one says "hippocampal volume." They say: he stopped recognizing me. She asked where her husband was, at his funeral. We lost him years before we lost him.

What dementia takes is not information. It is the person — recognition, conversation, shared days, dignity. And here is the fact everyone has overlooked: that loss happens twice. Once inside the brain, where medicine fights and loses. And once in the world — where nobody fights at all.

02

Why existing solutions are insufficient

Drugs address the biology and fail — and even in success, they only slow the curve. They cannot return a daughter's name to her father's lips.

Reminder apps and "senior tech" commit a design absurdity: they ask the patient to operate the tool — to remember to check the thing that remembers for them. Assistive technology for memory loss that depends on memory is not assistive technology.

Caregiving is heroic and unscalable. Families improvise the same crisis from scratch, guessing at what reaches the person, burning out at rates that make caregivers themselves a public-health population.

Story-capture apps and "griefbots" arrive after it is too late, and impersonate rather than preserve. They archive the past or counterfeit the person; neither keeps the living person present.

Every existing tool addresses a moment. But dementia is not a moment — it is a decade-long passage through changing needs. The failure is systemic: there has never been a system that walks the whole road, from healthy baseline through early drift, daily support, night terror, and what remains after. And there has never been a tool designed around the one constraint that defines this user: they cannot operate anything. The interface must be zero-operation — worn, not used.

03

The first-principles insight

Strip the problem to first principles and ask: where does a person actually live?

Not only in neurons. A person lives substantially in the world — in faces that are named, in days that are remembered, in stories retold, in routines kept, in being recognized back. Identity is partly external: a continuous transaction between a brain and its context. Dementia destroys the brain's half of the transaction. But the world's half — the names, the days, the stories — can be captured, held, and served back at the moment of need.

The disease and the loss of self can be decoupled. One no longer has to mean the other.

This is the same insight, applied to selfhood, that gave us every prosthetic in history. We externalized the limb in the 1500s, hearing in the 1900s, sight in the 2000s. Each sounded impossible; each became inevitable; each was dismissed as "not treating the underlying condition" — and each returned a life while medicine kept working on the cure. The self was simply the last organ nobody thought to build a prosthetic for, because until roughly two years ago, no machine could perceive a human day, recognize its people, and narrate it back in warm language. Now one can.

A second insight follows from the first. A prosthetic that watches a day to serve it back is also, incidentally, the most sensitive cognitive instrument ever pointed at a human being. Speech biomarkers — pause structure, word-finding latency, lexical range, sentence complexity — are validated early signals of cognitive change, detectable years before clinical diagnosis. A device worn through every waking hour turns diagnosis from a 30-minute snapshot every six months into a continuous signal against the person's own baseline. The prosthetic and the observatory are the same object.

04

Scientific and technical foundations

Remind is prescription AI glasses with three organs: a private on-frame perception system, a memory core, and a whisper interface. The system runs on a family-set daily rhythm — waking when the wearer wakes, resting when they rest, recording nothing outside those hours.

On-frame NPUA 14 TOPS neural processor in the temple runs transcription, face embedding, and redaction locally. Raw frames are destroyed in under 40 ms. Privacy is enforced by architecture, not policy — the cloud physically never receives a raw frame.
RecognitionConsented faces only, matched as 512-d embeddings against the family gallery. A face becomes a whispered name and a shared memory; strangers stay unprocessed.
Memory coreThe day's redacted events become structured memory — stored as embeddings with vector search (MongoDB Atlas), retrieved in milliseconds to answer "who is this?" and to write each evening's recap as a warm, specific story.
Cognitive WeatherDaily speech-biomarker trends charted against the wearer's own baseline — step-changes (vascular events) alerted same-day; slow drift caught months before a clinic could.
Care protocolsGrounded in dementia-care evidence: spaced rehearsal of fading faces (Memory Gym), music and familiar voices for agitation (Calm Protocol), night-time grounding (Night Guard), disorientation-triggered guidance home (Waypoint).
Consent layerFlagged private moments enter a family review queue — redacted by default, shareable with the clinician by choice. The wearer's dignity is a design constraint, not a settings page.

Every underlying capability exists today: on-device neural inference, face embeddings, real-time transcription, speech-biomarker literature, voice synthesis, and care protocols with decades of clinical evidence behind them. Remind invents no impossible physics. It composes proven parts into a category that did not exist — which is precisely what a prosthetic is.

The one-week prototype demonstrates the system end-to-end as an interactive simulation: the wearer's lens view (recognition, guidance, recap, redaction choice), the clinician's weekly report, the daily-rhythm scheduler, the review queue, and care profiles tuned to twenty distinct forms of dementia — from Alzheimer's to posterior cortical atrophy to CJD — because a real system must serve the whole disease space, not its most famous member.

05

Long-term implications

Diagnosis moves years earlier. When cognitive baselines run quietly in ordinary glasses, speech becomes the standard early test for cognition — cheap, continuous, personal. The window in which future disease-modifying drugs can actually work opens wide.

Research gets the data it has been missing. Four decades of failed trials share a root cause: patients are found too late, and early-stage longitudinal data barely exists. A consented, anonymized, family-owned Remind Observatory would be the largest longitudinal record of human cognition ever assembled — the missing substrate for the cure itself. The prosthetic that compensates for the disease becomes the instrument that helps end it.

Caregiving is transformed. Families stop guessing: what reached him today, what agitated her, whether the routine held. The sibling coordination, the 3am fear, the terrible question "is this normal" — answered by a system that was there.

The privacy model outlives the product. On-device destruction of raw data plus a human consent queue for what leaves — this is the architecture all always-on wearables will be forced toward. We would rather it be established by a device built on dignity than by one built on advertising.

The prosthetic generalizes. Stroke recovery, traumatic brain injury, and ordinary aging all involve the same double loss in milder form. Externalized memory becomes infrastructure — as unremarkable in 2050 as glasses for the eyes are today, and as unthinkable to live without.

06

The future this attempts to create

Every generation before ours accepted the same sentence: when the memory goes, the person goes. It was never questioned, because it was never a choice.

We are the first generation for whom it is a choice. The machines can now see, listen, remember, and speak gently. Composed correctly, worn lightly, and governed by the family rather than the cloud, they can hold a person's world steady while the disease shakes it — a daughter's name arriving in the ear a half-second before the panic; a day given back each evening as a story; a life kept, in the end, for the grandchildren who will one day ask who she was.

Medicine will keep fighting the biology, and someday it will win. Until that day, no one should lose themselves while still alive, and no family should be forgotten while still in the room.

Memory fades.
The person remains.
